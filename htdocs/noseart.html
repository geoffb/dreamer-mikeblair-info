Aircraft Nose Art

From the Kuwaiti War

 
Painting of the noses of aircraft, to personalize  the aircraft and to bring good luck to its crew, first became popular during World War II, and continued through out the Korean and Vietnam Conflicts. Although it can probably trace its roots back to the early days of sailing, when the proper selection of a figurehead, was not only said to provide "eyes" so the ship could see where it was going, but was also thought to bring luck to the ship and its crew.
During the Kuwaiti War the aircrew of  Carrier Air Wing Eight (CVW-8) and crew members of USS Theodore Roosevelt (CVN-72) revived this venerable tradition by painting the nose of the leading aircraft of each of the squadrons, stationed on board the carrier. During the heydays of aircraft nose art the most popular subjects were usually beautiful woman, with the occasional popular cartoon character of the day thrown in for variety.
